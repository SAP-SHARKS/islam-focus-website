Why I built it

Why I built Heemu.

It was a Thursday evening - that quiet hour after Asr when the light starts going gold.

I was sitting on the floor of the living room. My son Ibraheem - I call him Heemu - was next to me. He was one. He was at that stage where he'd crawl over, pull himself up on my knee, babble something I couldn't translate, and hold out a toy for me to take.

My phone buzzed. A friend in the WhatsApp group had sent a video - one of those short funny clips. I laughed. I forwarded it to my siblings' group, then to another group of friends I had studied Arabic with. I opened YouTube and watched a related video. Then another. The algorithm suggested a third. Then I was back on WhatsApp, scrolling through the reactions on both groups, trying to write a funnier comment than the last person. Someone replied with a GIF. I sent one back. The thread kept moving.

Forward. Back. Forward. Back. WhatsApp, YouTube, WhatsApp, Instagram, WhatsApp, YouTube again.

When I finally locked the phone, the room was darker than when I'd started. Maghrib was close. I had been there, in body, for sixty minutes - and somewhere else entirely.

Heemu was still next to me. But he had stopped holding things out. He was playing on his own, quietly.

Something in my chest dropped.

I tried to remember what I'd just watched. I genuinely couldn't. A funny clip. A reply. A thread about - I don't even know. An hour of my life. Vanished. And I couldn't produce a single thing it had given me in exchange.

Then a harder thought arrived, and it wouldn't leave:

If I gave an hour like that to my phone today, how many hours had I given it this week? This month? This year?

I did the math later that night, after Heemu was asleep. Roughly 1,200 hours a year on my phone. Maybe 30 hours with the Quran.

Forty times more attention to strangers than to the words of my Lord.

And that was the part that broke me.

Not the screen time. Not even the time with Heemu - though that hurt. It was the realization that I had been telling myself, for years, that I was "too busy" to read Quran.

Too busy.

For the Book that my Lord sent down. For the words I will be questioned about on the Day I stand before Him. For the one thing that will speak on my behalf when nothing else can.

Too busy - but not too busy for a banana peel, a GIF, a comment thread.

I knew, sitting there in that darkening room, that the word "busy" was a lie I had been telling myself my whole adult life. I wasn't busy. I was distracted. And every distraction was a small, quiet trade - Allah's words for someone else's.

So that week I built the only thing I could think of.

Not an app to block my phone - I knew I would just disable it. Not a Quran app I'd have to remember to open - I knew I'd forget. Something simpler. Something that worked with the addiction instead of against it.

Every time I reached for YouTube, an ayah would appear first. Ten seconds. Just ten.

The first day I was annoyed. I just want to watch this video. By day three I caught myself reading the translation. By day seven I was reading the word-by-word breakdown. By day fourteen I was occasionally pulling up the tafseer right there - at a traffic light, in the parking lot, in the small moments I used to give to nothing.

One month in, I checked my stats.

I had read almost two juz of the Quran. Without sitting down for a single dedicated session.

Two juz. From the broken minutes of my day. The ones I would have given to a clip I can't even remember.

I named the app after Heemu because every time someone downloads it, I'm asking Allah to bless him - and to bless every Muslim who has been quietly telling themselves they're "too busy" for the Quran, when really their phone is just heavier than they realized.

The app is free. It will always be free.

On the Day of Judgment, every small deed counts. Even one ayah.

Common questions

Is Heemu really free?

Yes. Fully free. No premium tier, no ads, no sold data. The only money that moves through the app is the optional ice cream charity payments - and that money goes directly to charity, not to us.

Does it actually block my apps?

Heemu doesn't block - it interrupts. You can always continue to your app. The point isn't restriction; it's awareness. (Blocking apps you'll just disable. Awareness sticks.)

What if I'm in a hurry?

Use an ice cream. You get 5 a day. They reset every morning. Need more? Buy them - that money becomes sadaqah.

Does it work in the background? Does it drain my battery?

Yes, and no. Built lightweight from day one.

Will my data be sold?

No. Ever. We don't even collect it. Your dashboard lives on your device.

What if I'm not very religious?

Heemu is built for every Muslim - practicing, struggling, returning. Especially the ones who feel they've drifted. You don't need to be ready to read Quran. You just need to be ready to read one ayah, one time. The rest takes care of itself.
